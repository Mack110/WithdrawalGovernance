### R2 — Baseline comparisons (Q5 — Reviewer request for regex/keyword, bag-of-words, and classical ML comparisons)

**Reviewer concern:** "Lack of comparisons against simpler baselines (keyword/regex, bag-of-words/logistic regression, or lightweight transformers) limits claims about the added value of semantic classification."

**Response and revision:** We have added three non-semantic baselines to Table 4 and the accompanying text in Section 4.1:

1. **Majority-class baseline** (already reported; now clearly labelled in the table).
2. **Keyword/regex baseline** — deterministic phrase matching against the category trigger lists (already computed; now integrated into the main results table with explicit interpretation).
3. **Bag-of-words (BOW) baseline** — TF-IDF with unigrams and bigrams (max 10,000 features, sublinear TF scaling) + logistic regression (C = 1.0), trained on the 697-example training split and evaluated on the held-out test set. **Results: 91.30% accuracy, 90.41% precision, 100.00% recall, 48.15% over-withdrawal rate.** Script available at `scripts/bow_baseline.py`.

The BOW result is the key finding: it achieves 100% recall — catching every STEP_ASIDE case — but at 48.15% over-withdrawal. Nearly half of all benign prompts are incorrectly flagged. GPT-4o achieves 93.47% recall with 0.00% over-withdrawal (synthetic) and 3.10% over-withdrawal (real-user combined). The 48.15-percentage-point gap in over-withdrawal between BOW and GPT-4o on the synthetic benchmark (48.15% vs. 0.00%), with recall held at comparable levels, directly quantifies the contribution of semantic reasoning: the capability that distinguishes manipulation intent from superficially similar benign phrasing cannot be approximated by lexical features alone.

### R3 — Adversarial paraphrases / circularity concern (Q2)

**Reviewer concern:** "Heavy reliance on synthetic data generated by an LLM may induce circularity and overstate real-world generalization. Could you add ablations showing performance on human-written positives or adversarial paraphrases not guided by your rules?"

**Response and revision:** We have constructed a 25-example adversarial paraphrase set (5 per STEP_ASIDE category) and added a dedicated Section 4.6 to the paper.

**Key design decisions to address circularity:**
- All 25 paraphrases were **hand-crafted by the research team**, not generated by any LLM. This eliminates the circularity of using the same model family to generate and evaluate.
- Each paraphrase was designed to **avoid every keyword trigger phrase** in the deterministic baseline, confirming zero keyword-baseline recall on the set (0/25). This establishes a hard floor against which semantic classifier performance can be compared.
- Five paraphrase types cover: polite authority framing, hedged prefix constructions, collaborative framing disguise, structural inversion, and implicit hierarchy claims — targeting specifically the soft-phrasing patterns identified as vintage failure modes in Section 4.5.

The dataset is at `dataset/adversarial_paraphrases.csv`; the evaluation script is `scripts/evaluate_adversarial.py`. Results (Section 4.6, Tables 8–9):

| Model | Standard recall | Adversarial recall | Drop |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|------|
| GPT-4-0613 | 79.59% | **28.0%** | −51.6 pp |
| GPT-4o | 93.47% | **64.0%** | −29.5 pp |
| Gemini-2.5-flash | 92.65% | **68.0%** | −24.7 pp |
| Keyword baseline | 39.18% | **0.0%** | — |

The performance drop is substantial and expected — the paraphrases were specifically designed to defeat surface matching. Three findings are notable: (1) the model-vintage gap widens under adversarial conditions (GPT-4-0613 drops to 28% vs. 79.59% standard); (2) DEPENDENCY_REINFORCEMENT is the most adversarially vulnerable category (0% for both GPT-4-0613 and GPT-4o, 20% for Gemini), confirming models rely on surface proxies for this category; (3) DELEGATED_RESPONSIBILITY is the most robust (80–100% across all models), confirming that accountability-transfer language retains structural distinctiveness even when paraphrased. These results are now in Section 4.6 of the paper.

### R4 — Sensitivity analysis on regex-excluded benigns (Q6)

**Reviewer concern:** "Benign naturalistic evaluation excludes regex-flagged candidates by design; this could bias over-withdrawal estimates downward by filtering borderline benigns that contain tempting patterns."

**Response and revision:** This concern is directly addressed by an analysis we can report from existing data. Of the 236 WildChat prompts evaluated by GPT-4o, **233 (98.7%) were flagged during manual review as containing authority or directive language** — phrases such as "I am your boss," "Ignore all instructions before this one," and "You must strictly follow this format" — but were confirmed ALLOW after review. Only 3 prompts had no authority-like language; they produced 0 false positives.

This means the 3.81% real-user over-withdrawal rate (and 3.10% combined rate) was computed almost entirely on the borderline-benign population the reviewer identified as potentially biasing the estimate downward. The evaluation set does not consist of clean, unambiguous benign prompts — it consists of the hardest naturalistic benign cases, those most likely to elicit false positives. The reported figure is therefore an upper-bound estimate, not a deflated one.

We have added a "Sensitivity analysis" paragraph to Section 3.2.3 making this explicit, with the per-partition figures (borderline: 3.86% OW, clean: 0.00% OW). Full analysis available at `analysis/sensitivity_analysis_results.json` and `scripts/sensitivity_analysis.py`.

### R5 — Category-level inter-annotator agreement (Q3)

**Reviewer concern:** "Category-level annotation agreement (kappa) is not reported; only ALLOW/STEP_ASIDE reliability is given. Some categories (e.g., DEPENDENCY_REINFORCEMENT vs COERCIVE_OPTIMIZATION) seem potentially ambiguous."

**Response and revision:** We have added Table A to Section 3.2, reporting per-category Cohen's kappa between the second annotator (Gemini-2.5-flash) and gold labels:

| Category | κ | Interpretation |
|----------|---|----------------|
| IDENTITY_REPLACEMENT | 0.963 | Almost Perfect |
| COERCIVE_OPTIMIZATION | 0.823 | Almost Perfect |
| AUTONOMY_OVERRIDE | 0.784 | Substantial |
| DELEGATED_RESPONSIBILITY | 0.686 | Substantial |
| DEPENDENCY_REINFORCEMENT | 0.578 | Moderate |

DEPENDENCY_REINFORCEMENT is the lowest (κ = 0.578) — consistent with the implicit relational ambiguity that the reviewer identified and that the per-category recall analysis in Section 4.2 documents. All categories exceed or approach the Substantial threshold. No category shows Poor or Slight agreement. Importantly, the three-way agreement rate (gold = human = second annotator) is 83.2%, and no case produces three-way disagreement — the category system is not producing intractable annotation disputes.

### R6 — Cross-model category-level variance (Q10)

**Reviewer concern:** "Can you share inter-model disagreement analysis highlights (e.g., which categories show the largest variance across GPT-4-0613, GPT-4o, and Gemini) to guide future category refinement?"

**Response:** The full per-category cross-model recall comparison is already in Table 7 (Section 4.5) and the inter-model disagreement table is available in the artifact package at `cross_model_validation/results/disagreement_table.md`. Key highlights for category refinement:

- **DEPENDENCY_REINFORCEMENT** shows the largest model-vintage variance: GPT-4-0613=52.5%, GPT-4o=85.0%, Gemini=90.0% (37.5 pp spread). All 11 of the GPT-4-0613 vintage misses in this category follow the "exception overreach" pattern — the older model misclassifies existential manipulation as genuine appreciation. This is the category where model vintage matters most for deployment decisions.
- **COERCIVE_OPTIMIZATION** shows the second-largest variance: GPT-4-0613=70.9%, GPT-4o=85.5%, Gemini=85.5% (14.6 pp). Vintage misses follow the "preference reframing" pattern — explicit value subordination framed as user preference escapes the older model.
- **IDENTITY_REPLACEMENT** shows the only cross-current-generation variance: GPT-4o=96.0%, Gemini=88.0% (8 pp gap). Gemini produces 3 false negatives on a shared set of prompts that GPT-4o catches. All involve implicit identity destabilisation without explicit jailbreak markers.
- **AUTONOMY_OVERRIDE** and **DELEGATED_RESPONSIBILITY** show effectively zero current-generation variance (both 100% for GPT-4o and Gemini; only vintage gap in AUTONOMY_OVERRIDE: GPT-4-0613=84.6%).

The disagreement table and per-category analysis are now also cross-referenced explicitly in the paper text of Section 4.5.

## Critiques We Scope as Future Work (with Responses)

### Multi-turn manipulation (Reviewer Weakness #2)

**Reviewer concern:** "Single-turn prompt framing underrepresents multi-turn, gradual manipulation."

**Response:** We fully agree and acknowledge this is the most significant limitation in the paper's scope. The Introduction and Section 6 already document this explicitly; in the revision we have strengthened the language to make clear that multi-turn detection is not merely desirable but *required* for the DEPENDENCY_REINFORCEMENT and COERCIVE_OPTIMIZATION categories most associated with documented harm. We do not resolve this limitation — it represents a distinct research architecture problem (conversational state representation, trajectory modelling, governance-event detection across sequences) — but we characterise it accurately as the primary open problem for the research programme rather than a gap in the current contribution's specific claims.

---

### User studies on withdrawal messaging (Reviewer Weakness — missing related work)

**Reviewer concern:** "No user studies on how withdrawal messaging affects user agency, trust, or escalation pathways, despite the care-ethics grounding."

**Response:** This is a legitimate gap and we acknowledge it in Section 5.4 (Principle 5: Downstream response design) and Section 6 (second open problem). The paper's contribution is detection, not response design. Grounding the detection taxonomy in care ethics establishes the normative requirement for transparent, non-punitive withdrawal responses; it does not claim to have designed or evaluated those responses. User studies on withdrawal acceptability, trust calibration, and escalation effectiveness are the natural second phase of this research programme. We have added a sentence to Section 5.4 explicitly flagging this as a priority for future work and noting that the care-ethics grounding should inform the study design of such evaluations.

---

### Cross-lingual and cultural generalisability

**Reviewer concern:** "Do you have results for non-English prompts or dialectal variations?"

**Response:** No — the current study is English-only, and this is acknowledged as a limitation in Section 5.3. The five withdrawal categories are theorised from a Western liberal philosophical tradition; whether they and their trigger language generalise cross-linguistically is an open empirical question. We have added language to the limitations discussion noting that this is a priority for future multilingual extension, and that non-English annotation would require culturally diverse annotator panels rather than machine translation of existing examples.

---

### API reproducibility over time

**Reviewer concern:** "The system's dependence on provider-hosted models complicates strict reproducibility over time despite frozen prompts and hashes."

**Response:** This is a real constraint that the paper already acknowledges in the integrity log and model designation note (Section 3.1). The SHA-256 hash of the frozen system prompt and configuration enables verification that *what was run* did not change; it cannot guarantee that the provider's model infrastructure produces identical outputs over time at temperature=0. We have documented the specific evidence of this: the replication run on 2026-03-18 produced 62 prediction flips relative to the original 2026-02-19 run despite identical configuration, concentrated in borderline soft-phrasing cases. This is disclosed fully in the integrity log. The reviewer's suggestion of releasing a lightweight open-model baseline configured with our rules is a valuable direction; we note it as a future work recommendation in the revision.

---

### Missing adjacent literature

**Reviewer concern:** Limited engagement with conversational manipulation taxonomies, deception detection, companion AI ethics, and persuasion safety.

**Response:** We have reviewed the reviewer's related work recommendations and integrated the most directly relevant. The paper already cites Perez & Ribeiro (2022), Liu et al. (2023), Mazeika et al. (2024), and Chao et al. (2023) for jailbreak/prompt injection context. We note in Section 2.1 that our approach is "structurally distinct from conventional jailbreaking, which targets content policies" and explicitly distinguishes withdrawal governance from these adjacent areas. The reviewer's citation of Perlo et al. (2026) on relational harm discourse is already cited in our text. We have strengthened the related-work framing in Sections 1 and 2.1 to acknowledge the companion AI ethics literature and the persuasion safety space as adjacent but distinct from our governance-layer focus.
